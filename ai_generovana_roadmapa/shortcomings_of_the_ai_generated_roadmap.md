# Issues with the AI-Generated Roadmap from roadmap.sh

## Overview

This document describes the main issues found in the AI-generated roadmap from roadmap.sh and suggests improvements that could make the roadmap more useful, practical, and easier to follow.

The current roadmap contains useful topic ideas, but it works more like a list of recommendations than a structured learning roadmap.

---

## 1. The roadmap is composed only of points, not a tree map

The roadmap is presented mostly as a linear list of sections and bullet points.

### Issue

A proper roadmap should show how topics are connected. The current format does not clearly show:

- which topics are prerequisites,
- which topics are optional,
- which topics are advanced,
- which topics should be learned first,
- how different skills branch into different use cases.

### Why this matters

A tree-based structure would help users understand the learning path more clearly. It would make the roadmap easier to navigate and would better match the visual style normally expected from roadmap.sh.

### Suggested improvement

Convert the roadmap into a tree-like structure with main branches such as:

- AI Basics
  - What AI can and cannot do
  - Common AI use cases in education
  - Limitations of AI
- Prompting
  - Basic prompts
  - Structured prompts
  - Reusable prompt templates
- Lesson Planning
  - Lesson outlines
  - Activities
  - Differentiated materials
- Assessment
  - Rubrics
  - Feedback
  - Practice tests
- Ethics and Safety
  - Privacy
  - Bias
  - Responsible AI use

---

## 2. The roadmap has no progress tracking

The roadmap does not provide any way to track learning progress.

### Issue

Users cannot mark topics as:

- not started,
- in progress,
- completed,
- needs review,
- skipped.

### Why this matters

Progress tracking is important because a roadmap should guide users over time. Without tracking, the roadmap becomes a static document instead of a learning tool.

### Suggested improvement

Add a progress system such as:

- checkboxes for each topic,
- completion percentage,
- topic status labels,
- estimated completion time,
- milestones,
- final checklist.

Example:

- [ ] Understand what AI can do for teachers
- [ ] Learn basic prompting
- [ ] Create a lesson plan using AI
- [ ] Generate a worksheet
- [ ] Review AI ethics and privacy

---

## 3. The roadmap does not mention any resources

The roadmap explains what teachers should learn, but it does not provide learning resources.

### Issue

There are no links or references to:

- articles,
- tutorials,
- videos,
- official documentation,
- example prompts,
- courses,
- templates,
- downloadable materials.

### Why this matters

Users may understand the topic names but not know where to start learning them. A roadmap should help users move from theory to action.

### Suggested improvement

Add recommended resources for each section, such as:

- beginner guides to AI in education,
- prompt engineering tutorials,
- classroom AI policy examples,
- assessment rubric templates,
- privacy and data protection guidelines,
- AI literacy materials for students.

Each roadmap node could include:

- short explanation,
- estimated time,
- recommended resources,
- practical exercise.

---

## 4. The roadmap does not mention any products

The roadmap talks about AI tools in general, but it does not mention specific products or platforms.

### Issue

Teachers are not shown which tools they can actually use for each task.

### Why this matters

The roadmap is meant to be practical. Without product examples, users may not know which tools are suitable for:

- lesson planning,
- quiz generation,
- presentations,
- image generation,
- writing support,
- student feedback,
- classroom administration.

### Suggested improvement

Add tool examples grouped by purpose.

Examples:

- General AI assistants:
  - ChatGPT
  - Claude
  - Gemini
  - Microsoft Copilot

- Education-focused tools:
  - Khanmigo
  - MagicSchool AI
  - Diffit
  - Eduaide.AI

- Content creation:
  - Canva
  - Gamma
  - Curipod

- Quizzes and assessment:
  - Quizizz
  - Kahoot
  - Google Forms
  - Microsoft Forms

- Productivity:
  - Notion AI
  - Google Workspace with Gemini
  - Microsoft 365 Copilot

The roadmap should also mention that tool availability, pricing, privacy policies, and school rules should be checked before use.

---

## 5. The roadmap lacks difficulty levels

The roadmap does not separate beginner, intermediate, and advanced topics.

### Issue

All sections appear to have the same importance and difficulty.

### Why this matters

A teacher who is new to AI may not know which topics are essential and which can be learned later.

### Suggested improvement

Add levels such as:

- Beginner:
  - Understand AI basics
  - Learn simple prompting
  - Generate lesson ideas

- Intermediate:
  - Create reusable prompt templates
  - Build differentiated materials
  - Use AI for feedback support

- Advanced:
  - Design AI-supported workflows
  - Create classroom AI policies
  - Teach AI literacy to students
  - Evaluate AI tools critically

---

## 6. The roadmap does not include time estimates

The roadmap does not show how long each section may take.

### Issue

Users cannot plan their learning schedule.

### Why this matters

Teachers often have limited time. Time estimates would help them decide what can be learned in one day, one week, or one month.

### Suggested improvement

Add estimated durations, for example:

- AI basics: 1–2 hours
- Prompting basics: 2–3 hours
- Lesson planning with AI: 2–4 hours
- Assessment and feedback: 3–5 hours
- Ethics and safety: 2–3 hours
- Building a personal AI toolkit: ongoing

---

## 7. The roadmap lacks practical assignments

The roadmap explains what AI can help with, but it does not include enough hands-on tasks.

### Issue

Users are not asked to complete practical exercises.

### Why this matters

A roadmap should help users build skills through action, not only reading.

### Suggested improvement

Add tasks such as:

- Write three prompts for lesson planning.
- Generate one quiz and manually review it.
- Create a differentiated worksheet for two student levels.
- Draft a parent email and adjust its tone.
- Create a rubric for a writing assignment.
- Compare outputs from two AI tools.
- Create a personal prompt library.

---

## 8. The roadmap does not include evaluation criteria

The roadmap does not explain how users can know whether they have successfully learned a topic.

### Issue

There are no success criteria or learning outcomes.

### Why this matters

Without evaluation criteria, users may complete the roadmap without knowing whether they can apply the skills effectively.

### Suggested improvement

Add “You should be able to” statements for each section.

Example:

After completing the prompting section, the teacher should be able to:

- write clear prompts with context and constraints,
- request outputs in a specific format,
- revise prompts when the first answer is weak,
- identify inaccurate or incomplete AI responses.

---

## 9. The roadmap does not address tool comparison

The roadmap does not help users choose between different AI tools.

### Issue

Teachers may not know which tool is best for a specific task.

### Why this matters

Different AI products have different strengths, weaknesses, privacy rules, and costs.

### Suggested improvement

Add a tool comparison section with criteria such as:

- ease of use,
- cost,
- language support,
- privacy policy,
- suitability for education,
- export options,
- collaboration features,
- school account support.

---

## 10. The roadmap does not mention privacy and compliance in enough practical detail

The roadmap mentions ethical and safe AI use, but it could be more actionable.

### Issue

The roadmap does not provide concrete privacy rules or examples of what should not be entered into AI tools.

### Why this matters

Teachers work with sensitive student data. Misusing AI tools can create privacy and compliance risks.

### Suggested improvement

Add practical guidance such as:

- do not enter student names,
- do not upload private student records,
- anonymize examples,
- check school policy before using AI tools,
- review data retention policies,
- avoid using AI-generated feedback without teacher review.

---

## 11. The roadmap does not include classroom implementation examples

The roadmap explains possible AI uses but does not show enough realistic classroom workflows.

### Issue

Teachers may still struggle to apply the roadmap in real situations.

### Why this matters

Concrete examples make the roadmap more useful and easier to adopt.

### Suggested improvement

Add sample workflows such as:

- preparing a 45-minute lesson,
- creating differentiated homework,
- generating quiz questions from a textbook chapter,
- summarizing student feedback trends,
- preparing revision materials before an exam,
- creating a project-based learning activity.

---

## 12. The roadmap does not distinguish between teacher use and student use

The roadmap includes both teacher productivity and student AI literacy, but these are not clearly separated.

### Issue

Teacher-facing and student-facing AI use cases are mixed together.

### Why this matters

Using AI for lesson preparation is different from allowing students to use AI in assignments. Each requires different rules and guidance.

### Suggested improvement

Split the roadmap into two main branches:

- AI for teachers
  - planning,
  - materials,
  - assessment,
  - communication,
  - administration.

- AI for students
  - responsible use,
  - research support,
  - plagiarism prevention,
  - critical thinking,
  - citation and disclosure.

---

## 13. The roadmap does not include examples of outputs

The roadmap does not show what a good AI-generated result looks like.

### Issue

Users do not see examples of completed lesson plans, rubrics, quizzes, or feedback comments.

### Why this matters

Examples help users understand quality expectations.

### Suggested improvement

Add sample outputs such as:

- a complete lesson plan,
- a worksheet,
- a quiz,
- a grading rubric,
- a parent email,
- a differentiated reading activity,
- a prompt library template.

---

## 14. The roadmap does not include a maintenance or update strategy

AI tools change quickly, but the roadmap does not mention how to keep it updated.

### Issue

The roadmap may become outdated as tools, policies, and best practices change.

### Why this matters

AI in education is evolving rapidly. A useful roadmap should be reviewed regularly.

### Suggested improvement

Add a maintenance section:

- review tools every semester,
- update school AI policies yearly,
- replace outdated resources,
- collect teacher feedback,
- add new examples and workflows,
- monitor changes in privacy regulations.

---

## 15. The roadmap could better support localization

The roadmap is written for a broad audience, but teachers may need country-specific or school-specific guidance.

### Issue

Education systems, privacy rules, and classroom practices differ by country and institution.

### Why this matters

A roadmap for teachers is more useful when it can be adapted to local curriculum standards and school rules.

### Suggested improvement

Add localization fields such as:

- country or region,
- school type,
- subject,
- student age,
- curriculum standard,
- language,
- school AI policy.

---

## Summary of Recommended Improvements

The roadmap can be improved by adding:

- a tree-map structure,
- progress tracking,
- learning resources,
- specific AI product recommendations,
- difficulty levels,
- time estimates,
- practical assignments,
- success criteria,
- tool comparisons,
- privacy and compliance examples,
- classroom workflows,
- separate teacher and student branches,
- sample outputs,
- update strategy,
- localization support.

These changes would make the roadmap more practical, interactive, and easier for teachers to follow.
