# Verification of AI Answers

This document serves as a practical guide for teachers within the **aipremna** platform. The goal is to help educators critically evaluate AI-generated content, ensure factual accuracy, and prevent the spread of misinformation (AI hallucinations) in classrooms.

## 1. Source Citation (Mandatory Referencing)
When using AI to prepare lesson plans or study materials, teachers must always demand transparency regarding where the AI found its information.

*   **Rule:** Instruct the AI to explicitly name its sources, textbooks, or official websites.
* **Example Prompt:** 
>*"Create a 10-bullet-point summary of the main causes of World War I for high school students. **Crucial:** For every single fact or date you mention, state the specific historical source, textbook standard, or official educational website where this information can be verified."*

## 2. Setting Guardrails for Educational Content
AI models tends to please the user at all costs, which often leads to them making up "facts." You must set strict boundaries and define the AI's role as a responsible educator before generating materials.

### Recommended Guidelines:
1.  **Define the Role:** Tell the AI to act as an experienced textbook author or pedagogy expert.
2.  **The "Honesty" Rule:** Explicitly tell the AI that saying *"I don't know"* is better than guessing.
3.  **Example Prompt:**
> *"Act as an experienced high school teacher and textbook editor. I need you to create a short reading comprehension text about math functions. **Rule:** Stick strictly to established scientific facts. If you are unsure about any specific detail or if the data is scientifically debated, do not include it. It is better to write a shorter text than to include unverified information."*

## 3. Cross-Checking: The "Double-Check" Method
Just like in a school staffroom where teachers consult with colleagues, the best way to verify AI content is to have a second, independent AI look over the work of the first one. This catches hidden factual errors and biased grading keys.

### The Manual Workflow:
If you are using free public tools, you can use this simple "two-tab" method:

1. **Step 1:** Use **AI Tool #1** (e.g., ChatGPT, Gemini) to generate the content (e.g., a history quiz).
2. **Step 2:** Copy the generated quiz.
3. **Step 3:** Open **AI Tool #2** (e.g., Microsoft Copilot, Claude) in a new tab and paste this exact peer-review prompt:

> *"I am a school teacher. The text below was generated by another AI for my classroom. Act as an expert pedagogical auditor. Review the material, list any factual inaccuracies or misleading questions, and provide a corrected final version."*
